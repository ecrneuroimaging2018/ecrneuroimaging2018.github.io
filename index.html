ECRs in Neuroimaging Day Abstract Submission FAQ

How do I sign up to give a talk or powerpitch?

Please send a title and one or two sentences describing the topic to l.quadt@bsms.ac.uk or k.themelis@bsms.ac.uk. Please state whether you are a postdoc or PhD student so we can spread talks evenly across slots.

What is the difference between a talk and a power pitch?

A talk will be a 10-15 minutes presentation with an additional five minutes for questions or discussion. A power pitch will be a 3-5 minutes presentation during which you can pitch your research to the audience. You can use max of 3 slides or just 1 slide stating your name and affiliation.

What should my presentation be about?

As we are a community of Early Career Researchers, we welcome with equal merit i) talks presenting recent results, ii) talks describing new techniques you are developing whilst in post and iii) talks outlining your on-going work with preliminary results. You can also choose to talk about a method that you think more people should know about, or the impact of your research.
In every case, we strongly encourage speakers to spend time putting their work and potential impact into a wider context for the audience, making the research accessible to those outside your immediate field. We want everyone to get as much out of the day as possible and gain a broader understanding of the work our community is doing. We hope you can take discussions into the very finest details during breaks and the evening!

Selection

We will be choosing talks to cover a broad spectrum of research, and will preferentially select abstracts (and therefore talks) that are pitched towards the broader neuroimaging community.

As a guide, abstracts should:

  • cover material using neuroimaging techniques (MRI, M/EEG, TMS/tDCS), or be related to the interpretation of neuroimaging results within humans
  • cover current research at Sussex (or for ECRs who have recently joined, recent research conducted elsewhere)
  • include definitions of specialist terms/acronyms
  • give a brief context of the research in the wider field

The abstract submission deadline is Monday, 7th May 2018. We will be in further contact with notification of who has been allocated a talk in the middle of May. Thanks for contributing!

Alternative neuroimaging-careers panel

Dr Sarah Fielding Smith

Research Fellow in Neuroscience (Neuroscience and Imaging), BSMS

Sarah completed her PhD at the University of Sussex in 2016, investigating psychological mechanisms of auditory hallucinations. She currently works as a Research Fellow at Brighton and Sussex Medical School, on an MRC-funded project exploring phenomenological, cognitive and neurobiological markers of voice hearing experiences in Borderline Personality Disorder. Her background is in Natural Sciences and Neuroscience, with clinical experience in primary care mental health. Sarah will be changing career path in September, undertaking her clinical doctorate in Psychology.

Dr Matt Thakur

Senior Insight Research Analyst, Wellcome Trust

Matt works at the Wellcome Trust as a Senior Insight Research Analyst. His specialties include Horizon scanning, Analytics, Data visualisation, Strategy development, Evaluation, Pain and analgesia, Osteoarthritis, Electrophysiology and Systems neuroscience. He completed his PhD in Neuroscience at UCL in 2011, working as part of the London Pain Consortium research labs.

Dr Natalie James

Professional Development Consultant and Coach

Natalie's clients include higher education professionals and academic researchers from all career stages from PhD to Professor, along with University departments and Higher Education Institutions that are keen to understand and respond to the needs of their staff and students. She holds a European Mentoring and Coaching Council accredited qualification in coaching and mentoring and is a member of the Association for Coaching. Prior to launching her coaching and consultancy business, she spent four years managing research staff development activity at the University of Sussex, before which she spent five years as a senior manager at the UK Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council. Natalie completed her PhD in Chemistry from the University of Bristol in 2009.

Dr Natasha Ratcliffe

Research Involvement Officer, Parkinson's UK

Natasha works at Parkinson's UK as Research Involvement Officer, coordinating the charity's Patient and Public Involvement (PPI) programme. Prior to taking up this role, she completed her PhD at the University of Nottingham, investigating multisensory integration and body representation in healthy participants and people experiencing somatic symptoms.
